Question

What is the relationship between the siting of natural gas power plants and poverty?

Key Findings

Communities within census tracts with natural gas power plants experience higher poverty rates than the rest of California on average. However, communities near wind farms experience a one-percentage-point increase in the average poverty rate compared to census tracts near natural gas power plants. The mean poverty rate for census tracts near natural gas power plants is 13.93%, while wind farms are 14.87% compared to California's average of 13.36%. However, there are double the amount of census tracts located near natural gas power plants (308) compared to wind farms (146). Furthermore, communities near natural gas power plants experience a wider range (51.16) of poverty rates compared to census tracts within wind farms (30.48).

Also, there is no correlation between natural gas power plant capacity and poverty rates (p-value = 0.49). Although a strong negative correlation (-0.22) exists between wind farm capacity and poverty rate (p-value = 0.006), we cannot say there is a causal relationship.

As we view the map of the power plants, we see that most natural gas power plants and wind farms are located in Southern California which may be due to the large amount of people living there.

Background

Research has revealed that gas infrastructure is disproportionately near low-income neighborhoods (1). The relationship raises a crucial question when discussing the siting of power plants: are these communities living there before or after a power plant has been approved for siting? While my analysis does not seek to determine which came first, it does delve into the poverty rate by census tract and the relationship between natural gas power plant siting. This analysis examines the relationship between California households living in poverty and those residing in a census tract where natural gas power plants are located. The Census defines poverty as families with income below the poverty threshold. In 2022, the official poverty rate was 11.5 percent, with 37.9 million people in poverty (2).

Natural gas power plants provide Californians with cheap, low-emission, and reliable electricity. They are also essential to ensure the electric grid provides electricity when solar and wind cannot generate electricity during low sunlight and wind. As of 2022, natural gas power plants in California generated 96 gigawatt hours (GWh) from the 39 gigawatts (GW) of capacity, making up 47% of in-state electric generation (3).

Although natural gas power plants have made up much of California's electric generation portfolio, burning natural gas is still burning fossil fuels. Natural gas power plants have countless negative impacts. Impacts include extraction of resources, transportation of resources, and local emissions. The combustion of natural gas results in emissions of nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulfur, mercury, and particulates (4). The resulting emissions harm people's health and the environment, contributing to smog (5). Illness from pollutants includes respiratory symptoms, cardiovascular disease, and cancer (6).

California Governor Gavin Newsome recently called for extending three natural gas power plants in Southern California, slated to retire in 2020 (7). The planned retirement of power plants using seawater-cooling techniques kills marine life (8). However, the power plants received three-year extensions due to rolling blackouts in the summer of 2020. Now, the extension has been extended again to 2026. The communities nearby have been vocal about opposing the extension, many of whom are farm workers. As California works to reaches its 100% clean energy goals, retiring natural gas power plants will be essential to ensure those disproportionately burdened by pollution receive adequate access to clean, pollutant-free electricity.

Methodology

I begin by examining power plants recorded by the California Energy Commission's (CEC) Quarterly Fuels and Energy Report, which tracks the capacity and generation of power plants operating within California or a control area with end users inside California (9). By grouping the energy source by capacity, we determine that natural gas power plants have the highest capacity out of any other energy resource. I demonstrate this with a table, bar chart, and a hexbin map.

Next, I utilized the Census API to collect data from the American Community Survey 5-Year Data 2019. I selected the Ratio of Income to Poverty Level fields C17002_001E, C17002_002E, C17002_003E, and population field B01003_001E. I estimate the poverty rate by dividing C17002_002E (ratio of income to poverty in the past 12 months, < 0.50) and C17002_003E (ratio of income to poverty in the past 12 months, 0.50 - 0.99) by census tract population (10).

With the Census data on poverty rates, I joined the geodataframe with California natural gas power plants and wind farms.

Using a kernel density plot, I visualize the distribution of census tracts by poverty rate for California, census tracts intersecting natural gas power plants, and census tracts intersecting wind farms.

Then, I calculated the average poverty rates by census tracts for California, census tracts intersecting natural gas power plants, and census tracts intersecting wind farms. I calculated the max poverty rate accounting for outliers for census tracts intersecting natural gas power plants and wind farms. Also, I calculated the count of power plants intersecting census tracts.

Next, I demonstrate on a point map the location of power plants colored by poverty rate.

I then created a correlation matrix to determine whether there is any relationship between the size of a power plant and the poverty rate.

Lastly, I used the Mapbox API to demonstrate power plants by poverty rate through a color ramp and capacity by circle size for both natural gas power plants and wind farms.
